I feel good? Why feel good solutions are no solutions

Raphael | Wed 20 May 2020

ideas

  • Clapping for Corona
  • Integration of transgressive situations
  • Heroisierung von „Opfern des Systems“
    • Durch Klatschen für Krankenpfleger werden diese als „Helden“ dargestellt, obwohl sie keine sind
  • der „symbolische Akt“ des Feelgood-Moments nicht als Lösung eines Problems (Krankenpfleger, Antisemitismus), sondern als Ersetzung des eigentlichen Problems im Diskurs
    • obwohl Antisemitismus ein bekanntes Problem ist und war, wird durch ein Solidaritätskonzert ein Narrativ an Zusammenhalt postuliert und damit das eigetnlich Problem ersetzt

It seems that at the time of me writing this, the trend has already passed and the dust has already settled. Whether this is because the need for those messages to be spread has reduced, granting less reach to them, or that I simply don't go out as much to actually witness these events, I don't know. The fact is, that the current and ongoing pandemic has acted as a collective crisis and thus created one particular need, that it seems we can witness each time there is one such crisis. It feels cynical to witness or even write about this, but it seems that for each mass shooting, ecological disaster or whatever crisis you want there is a collective need that arises from such a terrible event. Walk around at 9pm outdoors and you'll suddenly hear clapping coming from balconies and open windows all down the street. Remember how after somehow after each racist motivated mass shooting there seems to be a local concert promoting unity. Or remember how after the attacks on Paris on November 13th you couldn't unfriend Someone on Facebook without coming across their blue-write-and-red coloured profile picture. And while the degrees as well as the ways of this need vary each time, there is one underlying pattern that seems to crystallise. What such crises seem to produce might cynically be called the /need to feel good/. However, far from being the only explanation for such collective acts, there are implications from such acts that go beyond the mere hip cynical you‘re-just-trying-to-show-that-you‘re-a-good-person criticism.

So what does it mean when people are clapping and singing for their healthcare professionals in such a crisis, when their disadvantages have long been public and voiced? How can it be that, time and time again, in times of crisis, we as a society place so much weight on seemingly harmless but arguably effortless collective acts?

The quick solution to antisemitism

Several days after a terrible event in the german city Halle in 2019, wherein a singular shooter tried to force his way into a synagogue and killed two people, the city of Halle, in cooperation with local musicians [???}, organised a concert with the name #HalleZusammen („Halle Together“, a play on words of „alle zusammen“, i.e. „All Together“). Several hundred people attended the concert. And while my aim is not to single out people who attended or felt the desire to attend such a concert, I can't help but wonder what kind of message something like this can and does send. Not only does the question arise, who such a slogan and event might be for, but also who is it not for?
It is obvious that Germany has a difficult history with antisemitism as well as racism.
